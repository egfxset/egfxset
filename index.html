About

HOW?

All possible notes of an electric guitar were recorded in standard tuning using the instrument input of an Audient iD14 audio interface.

Using the interface output, the recordings of each sound effect were processed for recording using the line input of the same interface. The output levels and input levels were manually adjusted to match and avoid clipping, but both, the previously recorded clean guitar sounds, and the processed recordings were normalized.

Each guitar effect had different parameters with annotations that followed the next logic:


  1. The ratio of processed signal (dry/wet or depth) was 100%, except for the effects of delay where the relationship of the input signal with the delay was not perceptible, in which case the dry/wet ratio was changed to 50%.

  2. Those parameters that influence in time, such as delays or modulations, were adjusted audibly along with a digital metronome to 120 bpm. Some effects such as delays allowed the functionality of using a tap tempo to mark the delay time, but others, such as the Chorus pedal, can only define the speed of the oscillator that modulates the signal by means of a knob, thus involving an embraced margin of human error. In most cases, the annotations in degrees are approximations to the placement of the knob of each respective parameter and are measured counterclockwise. There is documentation in photographs of the parameters of the effects at the time of recording if a more thorough inspection is required on the position of the knobs of each effect.

  3. All those parameters that involved equalization filters were kept in a flat position, that is, without any type of affectation to the signal, leaving in all cases the knobs at angles of 90Â° or in a proportion of 50%.

  4. The nomenclature of the annotations in degrees was useful to measure the positions of each knob. Those that use percentages only indicate two values, 100% and 50%, making it easier to find the position of the knob in relation to its possible physical path. Features

All 138 possibles notes with each of the 5 pickups of an American Fender Stratocaster plucked by a 2.0 mm thick pick

Carefull annotations of each note, string and fret position, and the effect parameters applied

Analog gear effects with and without digital components
12 popular effects are divided into four categories: Gain, Modulation, Delay, and Reverb
3 different effects per category

GAIN

TubeScreamerMini: A compact version of the iconic TS-808 Saturation effect from Ibanez. The pedal used was made in Japan in 2019.

BD-2: A widely used overdrive pedal from Boss Company. The version used is a 2018 model made in Taiwan and has the same schematic as their first Blues Driver pedal released in 1995.

RAT2: A Procoâs very popular distortion pedal, the one used had the serial number erased but by its components and design we deduced that was a later 2010 model made in China by Neutrik.


MODULATION

CE-3: A Chorus Pedal made in Japan in 1987 by Boss. It includes, at its time, a ground-breaking stereo configuration, but we only used the mono one.

Phase45: A MXR phaser pedal made from 1977 to 1981, its a simplified one knob version of their well known phaser pedal Phase90, the one used was build in U.S.A. in 1980.

E-Lady: A Mooer clone of the Electric Mistress, an iconic flanger pedal from 1975 made by Electro-Harmonix. The pedal used was made in China in 2022, it features a filter mode that stops the flanger phase shift, but we only used the normal mode that imitates the functionality of the Electric Mistress.


DELAY

DL4: A delay stomp box unique in its kind when introduced into the market in 1999. This digital emulator of 16 different type of delays remained unmodified for nearly 23 years. Of the 16 possible delays we used three, Tape Echo, Sweep Echo and Digital Delay.The first one is an emulation of the Maestro Ecoplex 3, a solid-state tape delay created in 1972, and the other two where line 6âs own approaches to digital delays using 24-bit depth resolution, the Sweep Echo took the Echoplex 1 as base model but with the addition of a sweep filter thought the repetitions of the delay and the Digital Delay was a state of the art true stereo delay, but in this case, as in all the effects used, we only took into account the mono version of the effect. The pedal used is a Line 6 2007 model made in China.


REVERB

CR6OC: We used a digital reverb emulations of an Orange Crush Pro 60 12-inch speaker combo amplifier, the main reason was to introduce in some way the color of an amplifier and mainly, to do a common practice for getting reverb in the tone of a guitarist, we could have used different digital or analog reverb gears, but its sounding would have differ from the ones used in a live guitar scenario and would have been closer to a very dedicated studio tone, contrasting in one way with our latent space that is created only by mono recordings and with our main intention on comparing commonly used guitar effects with the results obtained of that latent space. The amplifier used is a 2014 model made in China and the reverberations it integrates are Plate, Hall and Spring digital emulations.
